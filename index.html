This map visualises the conditions under which collective trauma may emerge, persist, and transmit. It does not measure trauma itself — trauma is a lived, embodied, relational reality that exceeds any data model.

How to read this

A composite of conditions,
dampened by resilience.

A high Conditions Density does not mean a country “is traumatised.” It means the structural, historical, and relational conditions that can give rise to collective trauma are densely present there.

The Resilience Factors indicate where the collective capacity for mitigation, awareness, and integration may exist — where a society may have resources to process its history.

Before you enter

A quiet note.

At this stage, the map uses national state borders as a visual layer. But the realities of collective trauma often exceed those borders: statelessness, migration, displacement, regions, Indigenous territories, and tribal or cultural continuities frequently extend across and beyond nation-states. These layers are essential, but they cannot yet be fully visualised here.

The map is a starting point for inquiry, never a ranking.
